COURSE DESCRIPTION: Introduces students to the academic field of Women's Studies. Students learn about gender from a multicultural, multiracial feminist and global perspective. Emphasis is on women’s history; gender, culture, and nation; social institutions; sexuality, sexism and violence; and local and transnational women’s movements. 3 credit. Prerequisite: None.

Student Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to:

define key terms relevant to women's studies including sex, gender, feminism, and patriarchy.

describe and analyze the history of feminism and feminist thought as they relate to the development of women's studies as an academic discipline.

evaluate the social construction of sex, gender, and sexuality in American society.

assess historical changes in American institutions including the family, economy, politics, education, and religion as they relate to the role and status of women.

analyze the representation of women in the American media.

identify and evaluate various roles women currently play in the institutions of American society.

critically analyze race, ethnicity, social class, sexuality, and age as they correlate with sex and gender in American society.

assess the future of feminism and the women's movement in the United States.

Climate of Mutual Respect

This is a course about women’s lives that actively discusses sensitive topics such as race, class, gender, sexuality, and violence against women. The course will be run as a mixture of lecture and discussion. Regular attendance and participation is expected. Students need read the required reading before class and be prepared to participate meaningfully in each class discussion.

I encourage students to feel free and open to discuss a wide range of topics. That being said, please remember to be mindful and respectful of the opinions of your fellow classmates. We must all learn how to question, disagree, and debate without resorting to tactics of intimidation, harassment, or personal attack. Insensitive language, harassment, or disruptive behavior will not be tolerated. For further details on CSUDH’s Student Code of Conduct, see: https://www.csudh.edu/student-conduct/student-conduct-procedures/student-code-of-conduct

ASSIGNMENTS

Responses and Quizzes (10%)

1 – 2-page typed responses to the day’s assigned topic and/or readings will be due throughout the semester and are noted on their specific dates below.

Shero Archive Project (20%) DUE Thursday, October 13

The term “shero” was coined by Maya Angelou to refer to women heroes. The goal of this project is to recognize a shero in your own life. This could be a family member, friend, teacher, or mentor. This project should let you not only celebrate this special woman in your life, but also give you the opportunity to know her on a deeper level. This project is built in collaboration with the CSUDH Gerth Archives. Students will work with library staff to learn how to document and preserve archival materials connected to their Shero. Full assignment details are provided under “Assignments” on Canvas.

Activism Project (20%) DUE Tuesday, December 6

Activism is related to feminism in the desired goal to bring about positive social and political change. For this project, students will identify an issue for action, research feminist activist organizations related to your topic, and analyze feminism’s role in liberation movements. An assignment sheet with full details is provided under “Assignments” Canvas.

Title IX

Title IX makes it clear that violence and harassment based on sex and gender are civil rights offenses subject to the same kinds of accountability and the same kinds of support applied to offenses against other protected categories such as race, national origin, etc. CSU Dominguez Hills is committed to creating and sustaining an educational and working environment free of sexual misconduct, dating violence, domestic violence, and stalking. If you experience any of these forms of misconduct, you are strongly encouraged to report it to Elizabeth Schrock, the Title IX Officer and/or the police, as well as utilize the on and off campus resources available to you. Your safety and well-being is a top priority for the university.

Academic Integrity

CSUDH has strict policies against cheating and plagiarism. Students are expected to hand in their own, original work for all assignments in this class. Any breaches in academic honesty will be treated seriously. I reserve the right to fail a student for ANY (this includes rough drafts) assignment that is not properly cited, is the work of another student, or violates any form of academic integrity. For a full listing of the University’s policies and procedures regarding this, please refer to the Student Conduct Code: https://www.csudh.edu/Assets/csudh-sites/financial-aid/docs/code-of-conduct.pdf

As stated in the university catalog: “Students are responsible for the integrity of their actions and must be willing to accept consequences for these actions.” Among the forms of academic dishonesty addressed by the catalog are the following:

“Cheating--intentionally using or attempting to use unauthorized materials, information, or study aids in any academic exercise. . . . Cheating also includes: unauthorized multiple submissions, altering or interfering with grading, lying to improve a grade, altering graded work, unauthorized removal of tests from classroom or office, and forging signatures on academic documents.”

“Plagiarism--the deliberate adoption or reproduction of ideas or statements of another person as one’s own without acknowledgment.”

All writing assignments should be prepared by the student. Strengthening your skills in writing, analysis, and critical thinking are all learning objectives for this course, and developing strong competencies in this area will prepare you for a competitive workplace. AI-generated submissions are not permitted and will be treated as plagiarism.

AI Tools: AI tools can be helpful for brainstorming, outlining, and refining ideas, but academic honesty requires you to distinguish your original work and avoid any misuse that undermines learning. I, your professor, am here to help you navigate these new platforms all while encouraging you to maintain academic honesty.

✅ Do:

You may use AI to brainstorm, fine-tune research questions, and clarify your ideas.

Check grammar, rigor, and writing style with AI tools.

Fact-check all AI-generated content using credible sources.

Disclose your use of AI tools at the end of assignments (include the tool used and your prompts).

Engage with your instructor about how AI tools may be used in this course.

Respect others’ privacy and intellectual property when using AI in collaborative settings.

❌ Don’t:

Submit AI-generated content without proper acknowledgment—this violates academic integrity.

Use AI to complete your assignments instead of doing your own work.

Trust AI blindly—always verify the information it provides.

Use AI in ways that give you an unfair advantage or interfere with other students' learning.
