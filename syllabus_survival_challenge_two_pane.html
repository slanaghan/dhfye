Course Description

This course introduces students to the academic field of Women’s Studies. Students learn about gender from multicultural, multiracial feminist, and global perspectives. Topics include women’s history; gender, culture, and nation; social institutions; sexuality; sexism and violence; and local and transnational women’s movements. This is a three-credit course with no prerequisite.

Student Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  • Define key terms including sex, gender, feminism, and patriarchy.
  • Describe the history of feminism and feminist thought.
  • Evaluate the social construction of sex, gender, and sexuality.
  • Analyze how institutions influence the roles and status of women.
  • Analyze the representation of women in media.
  • Evaluate the connections among race, ethnicity, class, sexuality, age, sex, and gender.
  • Assess the future of feminism and women’s movements in the United States.

Required Texts

  • Susan M. Shaw and Janet Lee, Gendered Voices, Feminist Visions, 7th edition.
  • Additional readings posted in Canvas and identified in the class schedule.
  • Readings should be completed before the date on which they will be discussed.

Communication and Office Hours

Office hours: Tuesdays and Thursdays, 1:30–2:30 p.m. in LCH A329. Zoom hours are available by appointment.

Students should email the professor with questions, concerns, or requests for an appointment. Students can expect a response within 24 hours Monday through Friday and within 48 hours over the weekend. Most messages sent during normal business hours are answered within two to three hours.

Climate of Mutual Respect

This course discusses sensitive topics including race, class, gender, sexuality, and violence. Regular attendance, preparation, and meaningful participation are expected. Students should be respectful of classmates and learn how to question, disagree, and debate without intimidation, harassment, personal attacks, or disruptive behavior.

Assignment Details

Responses and Quizzes — 10%

One- to two-page typed responses to assigned topics or readings will be due throughout the semester. Due dates appear in the class schedule.

Shero Archive Project — 20%

Due Thursday, October 13.

The term “shero” was coined by Maya Angelou to refer to women heroes. Students will recognize a shero from their own lives and work with library staff to learn how to document and preserve related archival materials. Full assignment details are available under “Assignments” in Canvas.

Activism Project — 20%

Due Tuesday, December 6.

Students will identify an issue for action, research feminist activist organizations connected to the topic, and analyze feminism’s role in liberation movements. A full assignment sheet is available under “Assignments” in Canvas.

Exams — 50%

The course includes a midterm and final exam. Each exam has a Canvas portion and take-home short-answer and essay questions.

Time-management note: This is a three-credit course. In addition to attending class and completing course requirements, students should expect to spend at least six hours each week on reading, studying, homework, and projects outside of class.

Student Support and Campus Policies

Accommodation for Disabilities

Reasonable accommodations are available through the Student disAbility Resource Center. Students seeking institutional support should contact the center and communicate approved accommodations to the instructor.

Tutoring

CSUDH Tutoring Services and the Toro Learning Center provide academic support to currently enrolled students at no extra cost.

Women’s Resource Center

The Women’s Resource Center advances gender equity and an inclusive campus climate through programs, support services, and opportunities open to students of all genders. Students may attend approved events and submit a one- to two-page response for extra credit.

Title IX

CSUDH is committed to an educational and working environment free from sexual misconduct, dating violence, domestic violence, and stalking. Students are encouraged to use available campus and community reporting and support resources.

Academic Integrity and AI Tools

Students are expected to submit their own original work. Cheating, plagiarism, and submitting work created by another person or tool without appropriate acknowledgment violate academic integrity. AI-generated submissions are not permitted as replacements for a student’s own work.

AI: Appropriate Uses

  • Brainstorm, refine research questions, or clarify ideas.
  • Check grammar, rigor, and writing style.
  • Fact-check AI-generated information with credible sources.
  • Disclose the AI tool used and the prompts submitted.
  • Ask the professor how AI may be used in the course.

AI: Inappropriate Uses

  • Submit AI-generated content without acknowledgment.
  • Use AI to complete an assignment instead of doing your own work.
  • Trust AI output without verification.
  • Use AI to gain an unfair advantage or interfere with others’ learning.
